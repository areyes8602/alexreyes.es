Proof: derivative of $a^x$

Step-by-step proof that if $f(x)=a^x$ then $f'(x)=a^x\ln(a)$, starting from the limit definition of the derivative. The key idea is to rewrite $a=e^{\ln a}$ and recognise the fundamental limit $\lim_{u\to 0}\dfrac{e^u-1}{u}=1$.

Statement

Result to prove

For every $a>0$ with $a\neq 1$, the function $f(x)=a^x$ is differentiable on $\mathbb{R}$ and

$$\dfrac{d}{dx}\left(a^x\right) = a^x\,\ln(a).$$

As a special case we recover the derivative of $e^x$: when $a=e$, $\ln(a)=1$ and the formula collapses to $(e^x)' = e^x$.

Proof

We start from the limit definition of the derivative:

$$f'(x) = \lim_{h\to 0}\,\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}.$$

Substituting $f(x)=a^x$:

$$f'(x) = \lim_{h\to 0}\,\frac{a^{x+h}-a^x}{h}.$$

Step 1 — Factor out $a^x$

Since $a^{x+h}=a^x\cdot a^h$, the numerator factors as:

$$\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{a^{x+h}-a^x}{h} \;=\; \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{a^x\bigl(a^h-1\bigr)}{h} \;=\; a^x\cdot\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{a^h-1}{h}.$$

The factor $a^x$ does not depend on $h$, so it can be pulled out of the limit. Let

$$L \;=\; \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{a^h-1}{h}.$$

The problem reduces to computing $L$. Once we have it, we will have shown that $f'(x)=a^x\cdot L$.

Step 2 — Rewrite $a$ as $e^{\ln(a)}$

By definition of the natural logarithm, for any $a>0$ we have $a = e^{\ln(a)}$. Raising both sides to the power $h$:

$$a^h \;=\; \bigl(e^{\ln(a)}\bigr)^h \;=\; e^{h\ln(a)}.$$

Substituting in $L$:

$$L \;=\; \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{a^h-1}{h} \;=\; \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{e^{h\ln(a)}-1}{h}.$$

Step 3 — Multiply and divide by $\ln(a)$

We want to make the denominator match the exponent (that is, $h\ln(a)$) so we can recognise a standard limit. Multiply numerator and denominator by $\ln(a)$:

$$L \;=\; \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{\bigl(e^{h\ln(a)}-1\bigr)\cdot \ln(a)}{h\cdot \ln(a)} \;=\; \ln(a)\cdot\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{e^{h\ln(a)}-1}{h\ln(a)}.$$

The factor $\ln(a)$ is constant with respect to $h$, so it can be pulled out of the limit.

Step 4 — Substitution and fundamental limit

Let $u = h\ln(a)$. Since $\ln(a)$ is constant, as $h\to 0$ we also have $u\to 0$. The limit can be rewritten in terms of $u$:

$$L \;=\; \ln(a)\cdot\lim_{u\to 0}\frac{e^{u}-1}{u}.$$

The limit $\displaystyle\lim_{u\to 0}\frac{e^u-1}{u}=1$ is one of the fundamental limits: it is precisely the property that singles out $e$ as the "natural" base of the exponentials. In the standard definition of $e$, $e$ is the unique positive real for which the slope of $f(x)=e^x$ at $x=0$ equals $1$ — and that is exactly this limit.

Substituting:

$$L \;=\; \ln(a)\cdot 1 \;=\; \ln(a).$$

Conclusion

Going back to the expression we left at Step 1:

$$f'(x) \;=\; a^x\cdot L \;=\; a^x\cdot\ln(a).$$

Final formula

$$\boxed{\;\dfrac{d}{dx}\bigl(a^x\bigr) \;=\; a^x\,\ln(a)\;}$$

Quick check. If $a=e$, $\ln(e)=1$ and the formula gives $(e^x)'=e^x$, which is the property defining $e$. If $a=2$, the derivative is $2^x\ln(2)\approx 2^x\cdot 0.693$, so the curve $y=2^x$ has slope $\ln(2)$ at $x=0$, not $1$.

Comments and connections

Why this approach

Working with $a^h-1$ directly is hard because it does not match any standard limit we know. Rewriting $a^h$ as $e^{h\ln(a)}$ moves the problem to the limit $\lim_{u\to 0}(e^u-1)/u=1$, which we do know. This is the main use of the identity $a=e^{\ln(a)}$: to convert any exponential into one with base $e$.

Connection with the chain rule

Once we accept that $(e^x)'=e^x$, there is a shortcut: since $a^x = e^{x\ln(a)}$, the chain rule gives $$\bigl(a^x\bigr)' \;=\; e^{x\ln(a)}\cdot\ln(a) \;=\; a^x\,\ln(a).$$ The proof above is essentially the chain rule unfolded in $\varepsilon$–$\delta$ form: the substitution $u=h\ln(a)$ is the chain rule expressed in the language of limits.

Integrating the formula in reverse gives $\displaystyle\int a^x\,dx = \dfrac{a^x}{\ln(a)} + C$ (TANS 5.11), which appears repeatedly in exponential models without base $e$ (compound interest with bases $1.05^x$, radioactive decay with bases $\tfrac{1}{2}$, etc.).
