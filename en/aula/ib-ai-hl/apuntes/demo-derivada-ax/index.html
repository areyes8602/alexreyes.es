Proof: derivative of $a^x$

Step-by-step proof that if $f(x)=a^x$ then $f'(x)=a^x\ln(a)$, starting from the limit definition of the derivative. The key idea is to rewrite $a=e^{\ln a}$ and recognise the fundamental limit $\lim_{u\to 0}\dfrac{e^u-1}{u}=1$.

Statement

Result to prove

For every $a>0$ with $a\neq 1$, the function $f(x)=a^x$ is differentiable on $\mathbb{R}$ and

$$\dfrac{d}{dx}\left(a^x\right) = a^x\,\ln(a).$$

As a special case we recover the derivative of $e^x$: when $a=e$, $\ln(a)=1$ and the formula collapses to $(e^x)' = e^x$.

Proof

We start from the limit definition of the derivative:

$$f'(x) = \lim_{h\to 0}\,\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}.$$

Substituting $f(x)=a^x$:

$$f'(x) = \lim_{h\to 0}\,\frac{a^{x+h}-a^x}{h}.$$

Step 1 — Factor out $a^x$

Since $a^{x+h}=a^x\cdot a^h$, the numerator factors as:

$$\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{a^{x+h}-a^x}{h} \;=\; \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{a^x\bigl(a^h-1\bigr)}{h} \;=\; a^x\cdot\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{a^h-1}{h}.$$

The factor $a^x$ does not depend on $h$, so it can be pulled out of the limit. Let

$$L \;=\; \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{a^h-1}{h}.$$

The problem reduces to computing $L$. Once we have it, we will have shown that $f'(x)=a^x\cdot L$.

Step 2 — Rewrite $a$ as $e^{\ln(a)}$

By definition of the natural logarithm, for any $a>0$ we have $a = e^{\ln(a)}$. Raising both sides to the power $h$:

$$a^h \;=\; \bigl(e^{\ln(a)}\bigr)^h \;=\; e^{h\ln(a)}.$$

Substituting in $L$:

$$L \;=\; \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{a^h-1}{h} \;=\; \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{e^{h\ln(a)}-1}{h}.$$

Step 3 — Multiply and divide by $\ln(a)$

We want to make the denominator match the exponent (that is, $h\ln(a)$) so we can recognise a standard limit. Multiply numerator and denominator by $\ln(a)$:

$$L \;=\; \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{\bigl(e^{h\ln(a)}-1\bigr)\cdot \ln(a)}{h\cdot \ln(a)} \;=\; \ln(a)\cdot\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{e^{h\ln(a)}-1}{h\ln(a)}.$$

The factor $\ln(a)$ is constant with respect to $h$, so it can be pulled out of the limit.

Step 4 — Substitution and fundamental limit

Let $u = h\ln(a)$. Since $\ln(a)$ is constant, as $h\to 0$ we also have $u\to 0$. The limit can be rewritten in terms of $u$:

$$L \;=\; \ln(a)\cdot\lim_{u\to 0}\frac{e^{u}-1}{u}.$$

The limit $\displaystyle\lim_{u\to 0}\frac{e^u-1}{u}=1$ is one of the fundamental limits: it is precisely the property that singles out $e$ as the "natural" base of the exponentials. In the standard definition of $e$, $e$ is the unique positive real for which the slope of $f(x)=e^x$ at $x=0$ equals $1$ — and that is exactly this limit.

Substituting:

$$L \;=\; \ln(a)\cdot 1 \;=\; \ln(a).$$

Conclusion

Going back to the expression we left at Step 1:

$$f'(x) \;=\; a^x\cdot L \;=\; a^x\cdot\ln(a).$$

Final formula

$$\boxed{\;\dfrac{d}{dx}\bigl(a^x\bigr) \;=\; a^x\,\ln(a)\;}$$

Quick check. If $a=e$, $\ln(e)=1$ and the formula gives $(e^x)'=e^x$, which is the property defining $e$. If $a=2$, the derivative is $2^x\ln(2)\approx 2^x\cdot 0.693$, so the curve $y=2^x$ has slope $\ln(2)$ at $x=0$, not $1$.

Comments and connections

Why this approach

Working with $a^h-1$ directly is hard because it does not match any standard limit we know. Rewriting $a^h$ as $e^{h\ln(a)}$ moves the problem to the limit $\lim_{u\to 0}(e^u-1)/u=1$, which we do know. This is the main use of the identity $a=e^{\ln(a)}$: to convert any exponential into one with base $e$.

Connection with the chain rule

Once we accept that $(e^x)'=e^x$, there is a shortcut: since $a^x = e^{x\ln(a)}$, the chain rule gives $$\bigl(a^x\bigr)' \;=\; e^{x\ln(a)}\cdot\ln(a) \;=\; a^x\,\ln(a).$$ The proof above is essentially the chain rule unfolded in $\varepsilon$–$\delta$ form: the substitution $u=h\ln(a)$ is the chain rule expressed in the language of limits.

Integrating the formula in reverse gives $\displaystyle\int a^x\,dx = \dfrac{a^x}{\ln(a)} + C$ (TANS 5.11), which appears repeatedly in exponential models without base $e$ (compound interest with bases $1.05^x$, radioactive decay with bases $\tfrac{1}{2}$, etc.).

E5 Crane for fruit boxes — part (a)

The figure shows a simplified diagram of a crane used to lift fruit boxes. The bar $OBC$, of length $2\ell = 4\text{ m}$ (with $B$ its midpoint), is pinned to the ground at point $O$. Three fruit boxes of $25\text{ kg}$ each ($m=75\text{ kg}$) hang from end $C$. The mass of the rest of the elements is negligible. The bar is moved by a hydraulic cylinder of internal diameter $d_{\text{int}} = 40\text{ mm}$, pinned at $P$ and at $B$. Points $O$ and $P$ lie on the same vertical line, separated by a distance $\ell=2\text{ m}$. The cylinder allows the angle $\varphi$ to be modified, with $0° < \varphi < 65°$.

(a) Draw the free-body diagram of bar $OBC$. [0.5 marks]

Solution (a)

We isolate bar $OBC$ and draw all the external forces acting on it. The bar interacts with the outside world at three points: the pin at $O$, the cylinder at $B$ and the load hanging at $C$.

  • At $C$: weight of the boxes $W = m\,g = 75\cdot 9.81 \approx 736\text{ N}$, vertical and downward.
  • At $B$: force $\vec{F}$ from the hydraulic cylinder. Since the cylinder is pinned at both ends (at $P$ and at $B$) and only carries an axial load, it is a two-force member: its action on the bar is directed along the line $\overline{PB}$. By geometry (the load at $C$ pulls the bar outwards), the cylinder works in compression and pushes $B$ in the direction $\overrightarrow{PB}$.
  • At $O$: reaction from the pin. As a double support (pin), it has two unknown components: $O_x$ (horizontal) and $O_y$ (vertical).

The FBD looks like this:

Numerically. The weight has magnitude $W = 75 \cdot 9.81 \approx 736\text{ N}$. The cylinder force $F$ and the components $O_x,\ O_y$ are three unknowns, determined by the three rigid-body equilibrium equations: $\sum F_x = 0$, $\sum F_y = 0$, $\sum M_O = 0$ (parts (b) and (c) — outside the requested scope).

E·prop Tilting loading platform — proposed exercise

A rigid platform $AB$ of length $L = 3\text{ m}$ and negligible mass is pinned to the ground at $A$. A bag of cement of mass $m = 40\text{ kg}$ sits at end $B$. A hydraulic cylinder of internal diameter $d_{\text{int}} = 30\text{ mm}$, pinned between points $B$ and $C$, supports the platform. Point $C$ is fixed to the ground at a horizontal distance $a = 2\text{ m}$ from $A$. The platform can be raised at an angle $\theta$ to the horizontal, with $0° \leq \theta \leq 60°$. Take $g=9.81\ \mathrm{m/s^2}$ and ignore friction.

(a) Draw the FBD of the platform for a generic angle $\theta\in(0°,60°)$. [0.5 marks]

(b) Find the cylinder force $F$ when $\theta = 30°$. [1.5 marks]

(c) Determine the internal oil pressure $p_{\text{oil}}$ when $\theta = 30°$. [0.5 marks]

Solution

(a) FBD. At $A$: pin reaction with two components $A_x,\ A_y$. At $B$: weight of the bag $W=mg \approx 392.4\text{ N}$ vertical and downward, and thrust $\vec F$ from the cylinder along $\overline{CB}$ (two-force member, in compression).

(b) Cylinder force. Take a reference frame with origin at $A$ and horizontal $x$-axis.

Coordinates:

$A=(0,0)$,   $B=(L\cos\theta,\,L\sin\theta)$,   $C=(a,\,0)$.

For $\theta=30°$:

$B=(3\cos 30°,\,3\sin 30°)=(2.598,\,1.500)\text{ m}$.

Vector $\overrightarrow{CB}=B-C=(0.598,\,1.500)\text{ m}$, with magnitude

$|\overrightarrow{CB}|=\sqrt{0.598^{2}+1.500^{2}}=\sqrt{2.608}\approx 1.615\text{ m}.$

Unit vector along $C\to B$ (the direction in which the cylinder pushes point $B$):

$\hat u = \dfrac{1}{1.615}(0.598,\,1.500) \approx (0.370,\,0.929).$

The cylinder force on the platform at $B$ is $\vec F = F\,\hat u = (0.370\,F,\,0.929\,F)$.

Apply $\sum M_A = 0$ (sum of moments about $A$, counterclockwise positive). Only the forces at $B$ contribute, since the pin reaction at $A$ passes through the pivot:

$\underbrace{x_B\cdot F_y - y_B\cdot F_x}_{\text{moment of }\vec F} + \underbrace{x_B\cdot(-W) - y_B\cdot 0}_{\text{moment of weight}} = 0.$

Substituting:

$2.598\cdot 0.929\,F - 1.500\cdot 0.370\,F - 2.598\cdot 392.4 = 0$

$(2.413 - 0.555)\,F = 1019.5$

$1.858\,F = 1019.5 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \boxed{F \approx 549\text{ N}}.$

(c) Hydraulic pressure. The cylinder has internal diameter $d_{\text{int}}=30\text{ mm}=0.030\text{ m}$, so the piston area is

$A_{\text{piston}} = \dfrac{\pi\, d_{\text{int}}^{\,2}}{4} = \dfrac{\pi\,(0.030)^{2}}{4} \approx 7.07\cdot 10^{-4}\text{ m}^{2}.$

From $F = p\cdot A_{\text{piston}}$ we get

$p_{\text{oil}} = \dfrac{F}{A_{\text{piston}}} = \dfrac{549}{7.07\cdot 10^{-4}} \approx 7.76\cdot 10^{5}\text{ Pa}\approx \boxed{0.78\text{ MPa}\;\;(\approx 7.8\text{ bar})}.$

Order-of-magnitude check. Typical industrial hydraulic pumps operate between 5 and 250 bar, so 7.8 bar is realistic for a low-load domestic mechanism. If the platform is raised further (larger $\theta$), the lever arm of the weight grows and so does the pressure — that is why the operating range is capped at $\theta\le 60°$.
